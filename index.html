What is a Singularity?

In simplest terms, a singularity is found in the center of a black hole and marks "the breakdown of Einstein’s theory of gravity, general relativity" [5]. Understanding singularities is one of the most important things to understanding black holes. The tricky thing is that singularities cannot be seen from the outside of the black hole due to the event horizon so everything we know is strictly theoretical.

→

History of Singularties

The foundations of the theories behind black holes were first laid down by Karl Schwarzschild, a German physicist, in around 1916. He gave the first exact solution of Albert Einstein's equations of general relativity which "revealed the curvature of space-time around a stationary ball of matter" [8]. He realized "that if this matter were confined within a small enough radius, there would be a point of infinite curvature and density — a “singularity” — at the center" [8]. At the time, scientists thought that these infinite protperties were impossible. As the 1970s arrived, people found evidence that black holes exist this changed the understanding behind singularities. The Black Hole Initiative (BHI) group at Harvard has made recent progress on the uncertainty of singularities.

Diagram of a Singularty

Looking at this diagram, the entire structure would be defined as the black hole. Black holes curve space-time which results in the "hole" going downward as seen in the diagram. Once matter moves beyond the event horizon of the black hole, it has no choice but to move towards the singularity. This creates the single point at the bottom of the diagram where the space-time is curved infinetly. This point is the singualrity.

Properties of a Singularity

  • All matter moves towards the singularity
  • All matter that enters the black hole ends up condensed in the singularity
  • Infinetly dense
  • Infinite curvature
  • Zero surface area
  • Zero volume

Wormholes

  • A path through space-time connecting one point to a far distant point
  • Three types of womrholes: Einstein-Rosen Bridges, String Theory Wormholes, and Manmade Wormholes
  • The difference between wormholes and black holes is that matter can exit a wormhole but cannot exit a black hole
  • This difference means that a wormhole cannot have an event horizon
  • Some believe that they exist and other believe that they would break the universe and cannot exist
→

Einstein-Rosen Bridges

  • Said to be possible by general relativity
  • One side of womrhole time moves forwards, while the other side moves backwards
  • Things fall into a black hole and come out of the other side (the white hole)
  • Black hole connects to a white hole at what would be the singularity (A wormhole is not possible if the singularity is present)
  • It would take an infinite amount of time to cross, and the middle would seperate and close before something could cross

String Theory Wormholes

Very shortly after the Big Bang, cosmic strings were pulled lightyears apart, leaving wormholes spread througout space. This is an interesting theory becasue it gives way to the possibilty that we could find a wormhole somewhere in our universe while exploring space.

Manmade Wormholes

  • If an advanced civilization could create a wormhole and sustain it, they could theoretically time travel
  • Must connect one point to another that is a distance away
  • Cannot have an event horizon
  • Wormholes need to be big enough to travel through. Too small of a wormhole will have a gravity too intense for humans to survive
  • Find a way to keep it open: cosmic strings, exotic matter
  • Exotic matter: negative mass, negative energy, repulsive, somewhat antigravity
